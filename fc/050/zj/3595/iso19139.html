Abstract:

This map layer shows streams of the United States, Puerto Rico, and the U.S. Virgin Islands. The map layer was produced primarily from the Medium-Resolution and High-Resolution National Hydrography Dataset NHDFlowline feature classes, through feature selection and cartographic generalization based on reference to published small-scale ancillary data sets. This is a revised version of the August 2012 map layer.

Purpose:

These data are intended for geographic display and analysis at the national level, and for large regional areas. The data should be displayed and analyzed at scales appropriate for 1:1,000,000-scale data. No responsibility is assumed by the National Atlas of the United States in the use of these data.

Conceptual Consistency:

Measure Description:

Streams were checked for topological consistency using ArcGIS, to ensure there were no overlapping, intersecting, or multi-part features. ArcGIS topological checks were also used to verify correct spatial relationships between streams and county, State, and national boundaries. Streams were checked at coastlines to ensure their endpoints intersected the coastline. Selections by attribute and location were used to verify feature types based on their relationship to waterbodies. A geometric network of streams was built and connectivity was checked through upstream and downstream traces.

Evaluation Method Description:

Lineage:

Process Step:

Calculation of ReachCode and ComID In the 48 conterminous States and Hawaii, the ReachCode and ComID were imported from Medium-Resolution NHDFlowline. In Alaska, Puerto Rico, and the U.S. Virgin Islands, the ReachCode and ComID were imported from High-Resolution NHDFlowline. In cases where streams were added manually and where no ReachCode or ComID was available, a value of -999 was entered.

Date And Time: 2007-01-01T00:00:00

Process Step:

Feature Selection The Medium-Resolution NHDFlowline data served as the primary source data for streams in the 48 conterminous States and Hawaii. The High-Resolution NHDFlowline data served as the primary source data for streams in Alaska, Puerto Rico, and the U.S. Virgin Islands. Headwater streams in these two sources were selected if the same headwater was present in National Atlas Streams 1:2M, VMAP0 Streams 1:1M, or the IMW 1:1M. The downstream trace tool, a custom tool developed specifically for this project, uses ArcObjects to apply an algorithm to the indicated headwater streams to determine the preferred downstream route on the NHD geometric network. The tool analyzes the stream name, flow direction, and feature type (for example, stream, canal, or artificial path.) If there was only one downstream segment, it was included in the map layer. If there was more than one downstream segment, a set of criteria was used to decide which of the downstream segments should be included in the map layer. Priority was given to named segments, segments coded as streams, and segments with flow direction. If a downstream segment could not be identified with the trace tool, a segment was selected manually using EDNA Flow Accumulation data and USGS DOQQs to support decisions. After all possible streams were selected, the network was examined for connectivity. Connectivity was checked visually and by tracing upstream on the proposed 1:1,000,000-scale (1:1M) network.

Date And Time: 2007-01-01T00:00:00

Process Step:

Integration of Streams with 1:1M Coastline and National Boundaries The simplified streams were snapped to National Atlas Coastline 1:1M. Streams along the terrestrial international boundaries were snapped to the National Atlas National Boundaries 1:1M. Streams that form part of the international boundary were aligned with the boundary so that the two features are coincident. The data were checked for topological consistency using ArcGIS, to ensure that streams terminated at the coastline or terrestrial international boundary or were aligned with the international boundary.

Date And Time: 2007-01-01T00:00:00

Process Step:

Calculation of Name The name of the stream generally comes from the GNIS_Name attribute in Medium-Resolution NHDFlowline or High-Resolution NHDFlowline. Streams without a GNIS name were named if their upstream and downstream neighbors had the same name. Additional streams were named manually based on stream names in other small-, medium-, and large-scale maps.

Date And Time: 2007-01-01T00:00:00

Process Step:

Correction of Stream Density Disparities Selecting headwaters based on streams in the IMW map sheets created stream density disparities at the borders of the IMW sheets. Streams were deleted in the densest regions to create a more uniform level of density. Criteria for deletion included the type of stream, name attributes, and the length to confluence.

Date And Time: 2007-01-01T00:00:00

Process Step:

Cartographic Generalization All streams selected in the feature-selection process were exported as a new feature class, and then simplified using the Bend Simplify algorithm with a tolerance of 500 meters. The feature class was further simplified using the Douglas-Peucker algorithm with a 1-meter tolerance to remove any extraneous vertices. The new, simplified and generalized data set was then tested for network connectivity. A new regional geometric network was created, and upstream traces were used to search for missing or disconnected streams.

Date And Time: 2007-01-01T00:00:00

Process Step:

Cartographic Review - Phase 1 Cartographers reviewed streams by comparing them to existing small-, medium-, and large-scale data sets and maps. The name, location, extent, and feature type were examined and recommendations for deletions, modifications, and additions were incorporated.

Date And Time: 2008-01-01T00:00:00

Process Step:

Addition of the Intracoastal Waterway The Intracoastal Waterway was added to the streams, snapped to existing streams, and split at the coastline.

Date And Time: 2008-01-01T00:00:00

Process Step:

Integration of Streams with 1:1M Transportation Features Streams that were parallel to transportation features in the National Atlas 1:1M Roads and Railroads were moved away from the transportation features to ensure that there was a 300-meter gap between the two types of features. Imagery and topographic maps were used to guide placement of streams.

Date And Time: 2010-01-01T00:00:00

Process Step:

Integration of Streams with 1:1M Waterbodies During the generalization process, many streams became misaligned with the waterbodies they flow through. The streams and waterbody limits were corrected so that the streams flow through the waterbodies. This was done for all waterbodies except Swamp or Marsh.

Date And Time: 2010-01-01T00:00:00

Process Step:

Cartographic Review - Phase 2 National Atlas 1:1M map layers were reviewed together to check for features that needed to be added, deleted, or modified, and to determine if the distances between features were appropriate for the product scale.

Date And Time: 2011-01-01T00:00:00

Process Step:

Integration of Streams with 1:1M Counties Streams that are on a county line were aligned with the county line so that the two are coincident. Imagery and topographic maps were used to guide placement of streams. The data were checked for topological consistency to ensure that the alignment was accurate.

Date And Time: 2011-01-01T00:00:00

Process Step:

Calculation of Global Map Attributes The attributes GM_f_code, GM_hyc, GM_lit, GM_nam, GM_soc, GM_exs, and GM_loc are attributes for Global Map. They facilitate the use of National Atlas 1:1M data with other nations' Global Map data. GM_f_code was calculated from the Feature attribute using the following crosswalk: >Aqueduct, Canal, Intracoastal Waterway: GM_f_code = BH000 >Stream, Stream Intermittent, Artificial Path: GM_f_code = BH140 GM_hyc was calculated using the following crosswalk: >Aqueduct, Canal, Intracoastal Waterway: -999 (GM_hyc not applicable) >Stream Intermittent: GM_hyc = 6 >Stream, Artificial Path: GM_hyc = 8 GM_lit was calculated using the following crosswalk: >Aqueduct, Canal, Intracoastal Waterway: -999 (GM_lit not applicable) >Stream, Stream Intermittent: GM_lit = 1 >Artificial Path: GM_lit = 2 GM_nam was calculated from the Name attribute. If the name is null (blank) then the GM_nam is UNK. Aqueduct, Canal, and Intracoastal Waterway were given the value of -999 (GM_nam not applicable). GM_soc is the abbreviation for the United States listed in the Global Map Specifications Version 2.1. GM_exs was calculated using the following crosswalk: >Stream, Stream Intermittent, Artificial Path: -999 (GM_exs not applicable) >Aqueduct, Canal, Intracoastal Waterway: GM_exs = 1 GM_loc was calculated using the following crosswalk: >Stream, Stream Intermittent, Artificial Path: -999 (GM_loc not applicable) >Aqueduct, Canal, Intracoastal Waterway: GM_loc = 0

Date And Time: 2012-01-01T00:00:00

Process Step:

Addition of Streams Selected Based on National Atlas 1:1M Stream Gages Streams were added to the map layer if a gage located on the stream was included in the National Atlas Stream Gages 1:1M map layer. The new streams were integrated with existing streams, waterbodies, coastlines, transportation features, and boundaries.

Date And Time: 2012-01-01T00:00:00

Process Step:

Calculation of Region The Region attribute value is equivalent to the 2-digit Hydrologic Unit Code. It was calculated from the 4-digit Medium-Resolution or High-Resolution NHDFlowline Subregion code. Leading zeroes are not shown.

Date And Time: 2012-01-01T00:00:00

Process Step:

Calculation of From_node and To_node The From_node and To_node values were calculated for all features within the map layer using the Renode Polylines tool in the ETGeoWizards toolset. Values for the nodes are unique across the entire United States.

Date And Time: 2012-01-01T00:00:00

Process Step:

Construction of a National Geometric Network of Streams A geometric network was created for the streams. The direction of flow is the same as the direction in which the streams are digitized. The network was traced upstream to check connectivity. Disconnected segments were connected and line directions were reversed when necessary. Initially all features were enabled as part of the network. Some features were disabled because they are offshore, have indeterminate flow, have locks, or otherwise prevented the network from tracing upstream or downstream properly. The majority of disabled segments are either the Intracoastal Waterway or canals. NHDPlus V1 NHDFlowline, High-Resolution NHDFlowline, imagery, and topographic maps were used to guide editing in places where connectivity or flow direction was unclear.

Date And Time: 2012-01-01T00:00:00

Process Step:

Calculation of Stream Order Once network connectivity was established the RivEX tool was used to calculate the Strahler stream order value. Streams were processed by hydrologic regions or subregions and, if necessary, adjusted based on the stream order in upstream regions. Streams that are disabled were not included in the stream order calculation.

Date And Time: 2012-01-01T00:00:00

Process Step:

Harmonization of United States Streams with Canadian 1:1M Streams Along the United States-Canada Border Streams that intersect or follow the United States-Canada border were aligned with Canadian streams at the border in order to facilitate seamless cross-border mapping. This was a back-and-forth process in which each country reviewed the other's border streams, made adjustments to their own streams, proposed changes for streams in the other country, and acted on proposals for changes in their own country. Types of modifications included snapping ends of streams to each other at the border, reshaping streams near the border, creating greater separation between headwaters that begin near the border, adding streams if the corresponding stream exists in the other data set, and deleting streams if no corresponding stream exists in the other data set.

Date And Time: 2012-01-01T00:00:00

Process Step:

Calculation of Stream_ID The Stream_ID is the unique identifier for the features. The starting number is 10,000,001.

Date And Time: 2012-01-01T00:00:00

Process Step:

Integration of Streams with 1:1M Stream Gages Stream gages were snapped to stream segments. If the ComID and ReachCode for the stream gage did not match the ComID and ReachCode of the stream then the gages and streams were compared to imagery, topographic maps, NHDPlus V1 NHDFlowline features, or High-Resolution NHDFlowline features to determine which feature should be moved. Imagery and topographic maps were used to guide placement of streams.

Date And Time: 2012-01-01T00:00:00

Process Step:

Calculation of State Abbreviation and State FIPS codes The streams were intersected with the National Atlas States 1:1M map layer to transfer the State abbreviations and FIPS codes. The intersected layer was analyzed to determine which streams fell in multiple States. For streams crossing State boundaries, the State abbreviations and FIPS codes were concatenated with a hyphen to include information from all applicable States.

Date And Time: 2012-01-01T00:00:00

Process Step:

Calculation of Length The data were converted to a North America Albers Equal Area Conic projection. Length in miles was calculated for each line segment, then the data were converted back to geographic coordinates.

Date And Time: 2012-01-01T00:00:00

Process Step:

Calculation of Enabled The Enabled attribute exists for geometric networking purposes. The values are 1 for true (enabled) and 0 for false (disabled). The calculation of the Enabled attribute was done concurrently with the construction and verification of the geometric network (described above.) Initially all features were enabled. Some features were disabled because they are offshore, have indeterminate flow, or have locks. Other segments that prevented the network from tracing upstream or downstream properly were disabled.

Date And Time: 2012-01-01T00:00:00

Process Step:

Calculation of F_Code The F_Code is a numeric code indicating the type of water feature described by the feature label. F_Codes 4, 5, 6, 8, and 14 are found in the National Atlas Streams 1:2M map layer. F_Code 23 was created because there was no equivalent feature type in the National Atlas Streams 1:2M map layer. The following crosswalk was used to create F_Codes from the Feature values. >F_code Feature value >============================= >4 Stream >5 Canal >6 Aqueduct >8 Intracoastal Waterway >14 Stream Intermittent >23 Artificial Path

Date And Time: 2012-01-01T00:00:00

Process Step:

Calculation of Feature Types The Feature attribute describes the type of linear water feature. With the exception of Artificial Path, the set of feature types comes from the National Atlas Streams 1:2M map layer. The feature attribute was initially calculated based on the National Hydrography Dataset NHDFlowline FCode using the following crosswalk: >Aqueduct: NHD FCode starts with 428 >Canal: NHD FCode starts with 336 >Intracoastal Waterway: Coded manually >Stream: NHD FCode is 46006 or 46000 >Stream Intermittent: NHD FCode is 46003 >Artificial Path: NHD FCode starts with 558 or 334 > Since the waterbodies in the 1:1M data are not identical to the waterbodies in the source NHD data, the Artificial Paths had to be recalculated based on their spatial relationship with 1:1M waterbodies. Stream segments were split at the limits of waterbodies, except for swamps/marshes. Segments inside the waterbody were given the value Artificial Path. Segments outside the waterbody were coded as Stream, Stream Intermittent, or Canal based on the values of neighboring segments. Streams inside glaciers were removed. Imagery and topographic maps were used to guide decisions.

Date And Time: 2012-01-01T00:00:00

Process Step:

Integration of Streams with Version 2 of the 1:1M National Boundaries, Roads, Railroads, Counties, Waterbodies, and Gages Version 2 of the transportation, boundaries, and hydrographic features (coastline, waterbodies, and gages) 1:1M National Atlas data sets contain minor adjustments along the Canada-U.S. border and in other places where the data were improved or corrected. Streams were modified to remain aligned with these map layers. Topology, geometric network connectivity, and attributes were verified or recalculated for modified streams. Imagery and topographic maps were used to guide decisions.

Date And Time: 2013-01-01T00:00:00

Process Step:

Spatial and Attribute Corrections The addition of new streams, removal of streams, modifications to stream location, name changes, changes to flow direction, and changes in stream network participation were made to selected streams based on feedback from users of National Atlas Streams 1:1M 2012 and the Streamer stream-tracing web application. Imagery and topographic maps were used for guidance.

Date And Time: 2013-01-01T00:00:00

Process Step:

Update of Names and Reach Codes Stream names and reach codes were initially determined in 2007 but had to be updated to match the Medium-Resolution Flowline, High Resolution NHDFlowline, and NHDPlus V2 NHDFlowline, all of which have incorporated changes to names and reach codes since the creation of version 1 of the National Atlas Streams 1:1M. The ComID was used to link the streams with different resolutions and versions of NHD and NHDPlus. NHD has switched from using the ComID to using a Permanent_Identifier, but in most cases the value is the same as the former ComID. Streams in the conterminous States (Hydrologic Regions 1 through 18) were joined by ComID with the NHDFlowline feature class in NHDPlus V2 NHDFlowline, and the GNIS_Name and ReachCode from that source were transferred to the stream Name and ReachCode fields. If there was no corresponding ComID, then the stream retained its original Name and ReachCode. Streams in Alaska and Puerto Rico (Hydrologic Regions 19 and 21) were joined by ComID and Permanent Identifier with the NHDFlowline feature class in High-Resolution NHDFlowline and the GNIS_Name and ReachCode from that source were transferred to the stream Name and ReachCode fields. If there was no corresponding Permanent_Identifier, then the stream retained its original Name and ReachCode. Streams in Hawaii (Hydrologic Region 20) were joined by ComID with the NHDFlowline feature class in Medium-Resolution NHDFlowline and the GNIS_Name from that source was transferred to the streams. ReachCodes were identical for streams and the NHDFlowlines in Medium-Resolution NHDFlowline. No comparison could be made between High-Resolution NHDFlowline in Hawaii and the streams because they do not share the same ComIDs or Permanent_Identifiers. In all regions, in cases where streams were added manually and where no reach code or ComID was available, a value of -999 was entered for the ReachCode.

Date And Time: 2013-01-01T00:00:00

Process Step:

Calculation of M-values M-values were calculated for each reach using the NHD MValue Utility. M-values range from 0 to 100 and measure the distance of each vertex along a line. In this map layer, m-values describe the distance, as a percentage, from the downstream end of the reach. If a reach is composed of more than one stream segment, the range of 0 to 100 is not repeated on each segment but spread over the length of the entire group of segments that comprise the reach. M-values are stored in the m-coordinate of each vertex and are used in linear referencing.

Date And Time: 2014-01-01T00:00:00

Process Step:

Update of Stream_ID The Stream_ID is the unique identifier for the feature in the 1:1M map layer. The starting number is 10,000,001. It was initially calculated in 2012 for the release of the Version 1 streams. New streams and streams that were split into multiple features were given new Stream_IDs. All other streams retain their original Stream_ID.

Date And Time: 2014-01-01T00:00:00

Process Step:

Update of Strahler Stream Order Attribute The Strahler stream order attribute was initially calculated in 2012 for the release of the Version 1 streams. It was updated in 2014 for diversions, international border streams, and the Intracoastal Waterway. In border areas, it was modified to reflect the stream order of streams entering the United States from Canada and Mexico. For smaller international stream networks the stream order was calculated. For larger international stream networks the editors made a rough estimate. The stream order of diversions was reduced to 1. Streams that are disabled were not included in the stream order calculation. They have a Strahler value of -999 if they are inside the coastline. The stream order of the portions of the Intracoastal Waterway that are outside the coastline was set to -998 in order to aid in symbolizing streams in cartographic products.

Date And Time: 2014-01-01T00:00:00
