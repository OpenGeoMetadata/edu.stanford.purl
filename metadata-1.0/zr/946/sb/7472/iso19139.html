Abstract:

This raster layer is a georeferenced image of 1992 land cover for the conterminous United States, at a resolution of 200 meters. The map layer was compiled by staff in the National Land Cover Characterization Program of the U.S. Geological Survey National Center for Earth Resources Observation and Science, from data produced for the 1992 National Land Cover Dataset (NLCD). The NLCD used Landsat Thematic Mapper satellite data. This layer is part of the 1997-2014 edition of the National Atlas of the United States.

Purpose:

These data are intended for geographic display and analysis at the continental level, and for large regional areas. Process Step:

This land cover image of the conterminous United States is based on a land cover mosaic created from the 1992 National Land Cover Dataset (NLCD). The mosaic was supplied as a 1-band image with a 240-meter pixel size, in an Albers Equal Area projection. The land cover mosaic was projected to a Lambert Azimuthal Equal Area (LAZ) projection centered on 100 degrees west, 45 degrees north, with a pixel size of 200 meters. The data were oversampled to 200 meter pixels during reprojection, to match the resolution used for other map layers in the National Atlas, such as land cover and elevation data. Using the Arc/INFO Polygrid command, a 0/1 land/water mask was created for the 48 States at the same 200 meter resolution as the land cover data, using coastline data clipped from the National Atlas shoreline file. Before the mask was applied, a registration check was made between it and the land cover data. In most cases, land cover matched the National Atlas coastline well, but three problem areas were identified where land cover did not match the mask: >- Presque Isle, Pennsylvania >- Apostle Islands, Wisconsin >- Atlantic coastline of New Jersey, Delaware, Maryland, and Virginia. Applying a land/water mask to misregistered land cover data would obliterate valid data in the three areas listed above. Since most of the land cover data registered well with the coastline, data in the three problem areas had to be adjusted in a rubber-sheet procedure, Adobe Photoshop's Free Transform, to fit the coastline without affecting the remaining data. Free Transform is a graphical tool that enables image data to be skewed by means of pull handles on screen. Free Transform operates only on a selected area, and therefore does not affect pixels outside the selection. However, the "stretching and pulling" effect of Free Transform usually created a gap or seam in the data that had to be filled by cut-and-paste, so that the number of pixels per class did change for many land cover classes as a result of Free Transform. Summary of changes caused by Free Transform for the 22 land cover classes: >- The number of background pixels was reduced by less than .001 > percent. >- The number of water pixels was reduced by about .04 percent. >- Six classes showed no change at all. >- The remaining 14 classes all had increases in pixels, but 8 classes > grew by less than .01 percent, and the remaining 6 by less than .03 > percent. Compared to the data loss that would be caused by masking the data without any adjustment, the minor changes above were considered to be acceptable. Details on the adjustment process may be obtained on request. (The Arc/INFO Adjust command was explored but not used because it applied to the entire image and therefore pulled previously registered areas out of registration.) After adjustment, the land cover data were masked to set the values for ocean areas and non-U.S. land areas. Oceans and lakes are assigned pixel value 11 and non-U.S. land areas are assigned pixel value 95. The resulting file was converted to TIFF format using the RGB colors listed in the Supplemental_Information section.
